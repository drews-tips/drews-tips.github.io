How To Schedule Your Study for Maximum Productivity

I'm a bad runner. I'm slow, I don't prepare as much as I should, and I'm never anywhere near the front of the pack. In fact, I think I'm near the end most of the time. Fortunately, I treat running like others treat golf: I'm just trying to beat myself, not anyone else. Having that mindset has gotten me through a handful of very long runs, including about 10 marathons, a 50K, a 50-miler, and a 100K. There are some nice lessons that I've transferred over from running to all kinds of study.

Let's start with the premise: I've got a big interview coming up in a month. I always schedule big, important interviews way out. I do not cram. So, I decide to put in a study weekend. That is, a weekend where my only goal is to get in some serious study time.

One of the tenets in Cal Newport's Deep Work is the grand gesture. Newport tells a story about how Harry Potter author J.K. Rowling was finding it difficult to focus at home, so she checked into a hotel where she could work undisturbed. Getting away from the distractions of everyday life enabled her to finish the series.

With that in mind, I decide to get an AirBnB nearby to facilitate the weekend devoted to study. Making the grand gesture adds weight to the amount of focus. Why would I spend money and time away from my wife just to futz around on the internet down the street?

Now back to the running. One of the maxims in running is to fuel early and often. By the time you're feeling out of gas, it's too late. So prepare early. Given the proper preparation, you can go a really long time without having to stop.

Fortunately, the same foes for studying. Ever been a study zombie? You know the symptoms: foggy head, slightly dizzy, metal exhaustion, inability to focus. That feeling has stumped many would-be all-day studiers. What can be done? Fuel early and often. What does fuel for your brain look like?

  • Rest
  • The right foods
  • Exercise
  • Intelligent context switching

Rest

I'm a big fan of the Pomordoro Technique. It's got rest built right in. I extend the long break, and I add an even bigger one later in the day, and I stand by the effectiveness of the technique.

The right foods

There's something about cheese and most meat products that make me feel heavy and unable to focus, so I save those for celebratory evenings instead of days where I need to maximize the time I'm spending on work. Trust me: I would never turn down some good cheese :). Experiment with what foods get your brain going and which ones make you feel like you're thinking through mud. I read that salmon has a great affect on the brain, but I've never felt that to be the case (maybe because it's usually cooked in so much concentration-killing butter?).

Plants are my go-tos here. I have a green smoothie in the morning, eat as many apples/pears/grapes as I want when I start to get hungry, and have handfuls of nuts to supplement. Of course I eat real meals too. But on intense study weekends, I try to keep them light and healthy.

Exercise

There's something about getting my heart rate up that really frees up my brain. I can be a study zombie at 2, go for a 30-minute moderate-intensity run, take my time getting cleaned up, and by 3:15 I'm feeling ready to start up again, about 90% refreshed.

Engaging, restorative activites

Find a restorative activity that relaxes your brain. I find that these types of activities need to be slightly engaging: play music, read a fiction book, even take a shower, go for a pretty drive, grab lunch with a friend. I've had zero success playing video games, watching tv, or just surfing the web. Those seem to drain more of my energy.

Note: playing music as rest is interesting to me as a former musician. The idea of setting down my clarinet after 2 hours of intense practice and picking up another instrument doesn't seem restorative. But, after 2 hours of studying algorithms and data structures, picking up my bass feels pretty good. I assume that it has to do with the type of mental gymnastics you do in each field and that the switching is the important part, not the destination.

Schedule for optimizing productivity

7:00 - 8:00 -- Leisurely wake up, eat, get ready for the day
8:00 - 10:00 -- 4 study pomodoros
10:00 - 10:45 -- long break with an engaging, restorative activity
10:45 - 12:45 -- 4 study pomodoros
12:45 - 2:00 -- Lunch and another break
2:00 - 3:00 -- 2 pomodoros
3:00 - 4:30 -- Exercise and clean up
4:30 - 5:30 -- 2 pomodoros
5:30 - 8:00 -- Dinner with wife or friends
8:00 - 9:00 -- 2 pomodoros
9:00 - 9:30 -- Break
9:30 - 10:00 -- 1 pomodoro
10:00 - 11:00 -- Relax and get ready for bed
11:00 -- Sleep

Total: 15 pomodoros

Comments

This schedule will maximize your focus and productivity for as long as possible. Notice the final total; it's not even 7.5 hours worth of actual work. You'll actually be doing less using this system, than trying to grind the night away, unfocused hour after unfocused hour.

This schedule was constructed from experience. I ground away dumb hours for years as a musician. It took years to realize what I was doing wrong. I regret not being more intentional in my day-to-day planning. Here's what those days looked like:

Schedule for not maximizing productivity

7:00 - 8:00 -- Wake up and get ready
8:00 - 10:00 -- Grind away. No breaks
10:00 - 10:30 -- Break
10:30 - 12:30 -- 2 more hours of work
12:30 - 2:00 -- Lunch
2:00 - 4:00 -- 2 more hours
4:00 - 4:30 -- Break
4:30 - 6:30 -- 2 more hours

Can you see the problems? The breaks are arbitrary. They are just after 2 hours of work. They aren't intended to maximize focus. They're just when the clock strikes. Contrast that with the maximized schedule. Tons of breaks.

Using running again, imagine sprinting for as long as you can. You'll be tired, and then you'll take a break. The next time you go to sprint, you won't make it as far. As the day wears on, your sprints will get shorter and shorter until you're just shuffling along. Physically shuffling along is the same as being a study zombie. It's a little hard to tell because you aren't breathing heavily and your heart rate will only be slightly elevated, but you're shuffling, and you're not making progress. You might even mentally stumble and start doing the wrong things, essentially walking backward.

One last analogy: I did a pushup challenge a few months ago. The idea was to do as many pushups in the day as possible. Given these schedules, how you would go about that? I'll tell you what I did. I did 10 pushups every half-hour, and since they were so spread out, I was able to maintain that pace for the entire day. It would've been embarrassing to do them in a meeting, so I missed a couple of sessions in there, but I counted 300 pushups at the end of the day.

I can imagine just grinding them out in front of the TV after dinner for a couple of hours, but that doesn't sound like fun.

And it's just not sustainable.
