We'll cover the final 2 together:

  1. What's your goal?
  2. What materials are you going to use to achieve that goal?
  3. How have others achieved this goal?
  4. How best can you simulate part (or all) of what you want to be able to do?
  5. How much time and how often are you going to work on achieving this goal?
  6. How will you receive feedback?
  7. How will you incorporate feedback into your work?

Final stretch!

Feedback comes in all forms, and it doesn't have to come from another person. If you're studying for a big test, you might have answers in the back of the book that hopefully come with explanations.

Here are the sources of feedback, ranked from good to bad:

  • Master familiar with your work
  • Master not familiar with your work or someone pretty good who's familiar with your work
  • Someone pretty good who's not familiar with your work
  • General audience
  • Non-human source that's awesome
  • Non-human source that's ok
  • Public speaker
  • Non-human source that's bad

That list reveals 2 large categories: human and non-human with varying degrees of success. I'll talk about why I've organized the list this way and what it potentially means.

Master familiar with your work

Think of every hero's journey story out there: Star Wars, Harry Potter, Avatar: The Last Airbender, The Lord of the Rings, etc. The master knows exactly what's wrong with the subject and can help him/her improve. This is built-in in music: we have private lessons almost from day 1. (Tech doesn't really do this, which surprised me. I think it's opportunity-cost driven in that senior devs need to be writing code and not focusing a bulk of their time on actually teaching.) The master knows where you're weak and knows how to help you get better.

Master not familiar with your work or someone pretty good who's familiar with your work

This would be like going to a music conference and taking a one-time masterclass with a pro. S/He can point out the biggest flaws and maybe a method of overcoming them but has no idea of anything deeper. On the business side, image asking for a review of your work from someone really good but from another department that you don't work with all the time.

Balancing out this one are the people that are pretty good but that you see all the time. Think "master minus," that is, still good, but not as good.

The above are the ideal solutions, but most of us have to deal with these down here

Someone pretty good who's not familiar with your work

These are the people that most of use work with all the time. They were in our training class when we started our new job or our first classes together in school. They're valuable resources, but they aren't going to be as effective as those above. The bonus is that they're usually free. All those above usually cost a ton to work with.

General audience

This is what I call blind feedback. No one's going to give you any direct words to improve, but you're going to learn from the experience. Examples are practicing your recital materials in front of a group of middle-school students. You'll get a feel for when they're bored really fast, and maybe that means you need to change something up.

It could also be an annual performance review from your boss's boss's boss. That person has no clue about your work specifically but knows the general effect you're having on your team and the office in total.

These are valuable for the overall impression you're giving, but offer zero literal advice.

Non-human source that's awesome

The best feedback is specific and actionable. By the time you get back to your desk, you know exactly what you're going to do differently. When you get away from humans, it's pretty tough to figure that out.

Feedback in this category comes from celebrated tomes in your industry. Cracking the Coding Interview for tech interviewing, Clean Code for writing code, 48 Studies by Ferling if you're an oboist or saxophonist, Patterns for Jazz if you're diving into improvisation. Whatever the biggie is for your field, that's the best way to determine what you should be able to do. But it's not going to tell you how, just what.

Now we're getting into the categories to avoid

Non-human source that's ok

These are pamphlets written by ok teachers and books with rigid rules that don't understand any kind of nuance or subtlety. Most personality tests and book centered around them fall into this category (think Myers-Briggs). Avoid.

Public speaker

This is perhaps the most-pernicious of them all. These are the people who offer nothing by vague platitudes: "Follow your passion" is probably the most-damaging of them all. I, for example, followed my passion to be Beeding Gums Murphy until I was almost 30 years old.

  • Follow passion

But following my passion left me completely closed to other things I might have enjoyed: like computer science, economics, and psychology. I loved studying those, but I mostly ignored them, because I was so passion driven. That was a mistake. You know what would've been some good advice, that's waaaaaay less sexy than "follow your passion"? Pretty much everything in Cal Newport's So Good They Can't Ignore You.

The problem with public speakers is that they give what I call "lazy advice." It's easy to tell a crowd to "work hard," but what does that really mean? If we plugged "work hard" into a SMART goal-setting system, it doesn't meet any of the criteria for a good goal. But, it's very easy to say.

Non-human source that's bad

Don't follow bad books.

So, how does all this matter to my case-study goal of learning a new codebase?

It helps me guide my thinking on where I want to get feedback from. Starting from the bottom, I probably should avoid advice from developers worse than I am and inspiring speeches about the "beauty of code." They probably aren't bad or intended to be harmful, but they have a much lower chance of actually being helpful.

Can I find an expert familiar with my work? Not yet. I'm too new to the team and the company. Given that software development is largely an oral tradition, I think that'll come along once I get more time in my new space.

Getting a master or someone pretty good to look at my work is probably what's going to happen for a while. That's perfect. I'll be able to learn a ton from people like that.

I'll also submit my work to a pool of developers (General Audience) to see what they think of it as a whole. They'll toss out random bits of help that will guide me to fitting in and then being able to reach out to others for more help.

In this new situation, I'm generally going to avoid reading a really good book, in place of getting actual feedback. That may sound contradictory But Drew, why would you ever want to ignore advice from a proven source of information?

Because there's an opportunity cost to spending time reading Clean Code versus actually writing code in a new system. Given my north star: 1-3 tasks completed each week for my first 4 weeks on the team, ramping up my Clean Code skills is not the best step forward, albeit still a good one.

My feedback plans are also driven by my north star, so I should be good to go.

... and if not, that's ok; I'll course-correct as soon as I find out :)
