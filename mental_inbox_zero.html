Mental Inbox Zero

David Allen's Getting Things Done is a very famous productivity book. It taught me a number of things, and only one of them is about productivity.

At first glance, it has all the trappings of a normal self-help book: charts, a solution to a nagging problem you've had, glowing recommendations from luminaries in the productivity field. But I always had one big problem with the author's system. It's just too complicated.

The first time I laid eyes on that flow chart, I knew that I wouldn't be able to do anything with the book. How could something intended to simplify make things so much more complex? Would I really need to go through that decision matrix every time I needed to accomplish something? No thanks.

Instead, I learned two things from the book: one the author meant to convey and one he didn't.

1. For a book to be "good" and worth recommending, it needs to affect my life in only one way.

It's not that I lowered my standards. It's just that life is complicated and full of tons of things to do and learn, so I set the bar really low. Like, read-a-200-page-book-and-only-take-1-thing-from-it-low.

Making the standard receiving any life improvement from a book changed how I evaluate them.

2. Get things off your plate as soon as possible.

That was so huge for me that when I first dug into the book in 2011, it immediately changed how I lived. To paraphrase the author, if a task comes across your plate and you can knock it out in under 2 minutes, just do it.

I combine this with Cal Newport's How To Become a Straight-A Student's idea of scheduling everything to free my mind from tasks that take longer than 2 minutes. If it doesn't get scheduled, it's not getting done.

Huge.

I didn't believe it at first, but the secondary effect of removing these nuisances is that you never have to keep anything in your head. Nothing burdens your working memory. It's free to think about the next big, exciting thing instead of trying to remember that you've got an appointment in a few hours.

I call this "mental inbox zero," and I make use of it all the time to increase my focus. For example, I was working earlier, and I was in the middle of a Pomodoro. Those 25 minutes are sacred. But, I had another thought come up that was distracting me, so I popped open a sticky note on my Mac and wrote it down. That freed my mind to focus more. Similarly, I keep bookmarks of different notepads on my browser, just one click away, so that I can dump ideas into them without having to be distracted when I'm in the middle of working.

Another example in a Pomodoro: I had to drop my car off at the shop today (darn "check engine" light), and I was pretty sure that I had to be there at 12:30... or was it 11:30? 1:30? I couldn't remember, and it was distracting to try and remember while I was working. Remembering the 2-minute rule, I checked my calendar, saw that the appointment was for 12:30, and set a timer on my phone to go off 30 minutes beforehand. I no longer needed to think about anything except my work. My mind was free to dive back into productivity.

One of my favorite Cal Newport thoughts is:

High-Quality Work Produced = Time Spent x Intensity of Focus

I want to keep the intensity of my focus until my Pomodoro timer goes off. It's just 25 minutes, and I want to make the most of it.
